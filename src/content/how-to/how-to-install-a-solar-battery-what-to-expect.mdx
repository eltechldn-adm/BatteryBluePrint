Installation day is exciting — you're about to have a battery system that will power your home through outages and cut your electricity bills. But if you've never had a battery installed before, you might not know what to expect, what to prepare, or how to tell if the job has been done correctly.

This guide walks you through the entire installation process from the day before to the day after, so you can be a confident, informed homeowner throughout.

## Before Installation Day: What to Prepare

A smooth installation starts with good preparation. Here's what to do in the days before your installer arrives.

### Clear the Installation Area

Your installer will need clear access to the installation location — typically a garage wall, utility room, or exterior wall. Remove any items stored in front of the planned location. Leave at least 1 metre of clearance in front of the battery for the installer to work safely.

If the battery is going outdoors, make sure the area is accessible and that any gates or side passages are unlocked.

### Arrange for an Adult to Be Home

Someone over 18 must be present for the entire installation. The installer will need to ask questions, get sign-offs, and walk you through the system at the end. This is not a job you can leave running while you go to work.

Most installations take 4–8 hours. Plan for a full day at home.

### Know Where Your Main Switchboard Is

The installer will need access to your main electrical switchboard (also called the consumer unit, breaker box, or distribution board). Know where it is and make sure it's accessible. If it's in a locked cupboard, have the key ready.

### Confirm the Quote Details

Before the installer arrives, re-read your quote and confirm:
- The battery brand and model being installed
- The total installed price (including any switchboard work)
- Whether permits have been obtained (ask for permit numbers if applicable)
- The warranty terms

If anything has changed since the quote was issued, discuss it before work begins — not after.

## What Happens During Installation

### Stage 1: Site Assessment and Planning (30–60 minutes)

The installer's first job is to assess the actual installation site and plan the cable routes. Even if they visited for the quote, they'll do a more detailed assessment on installation day.

They'll check:
- The condition of your switchboard and whether it needs upgrading
- The best cable route from the battery location to the switchboard
- The structural integrity of the wall where the battery will be mounted
- Whether any additional earthing or protection devices are needed

This is a good time to ask any questions you have about the process.

### Stage 2: Switchboard Work (1–3 hours)

Most battery installations require modifications to your electrical switchboard. This is the most technically complex part of the job and must be done by a licensed electrician.

Typical switchboard work includes:
- Installing a new circuit breaker for the battery
- Adding a backup circuit (if you want a dedicated backup power circuit for essential loads)
- Installing an energy meter (if not already present)
- Adding surge protection devices

If your switchboard is old or doesn't have spare capacity, it may need to be upgraded. This should have been identified at the quote stage, but occasionally surprises emerge on installation day. If the installer identifies additional work needed, get a written explanation and revised price before agreeing to proceed.

### Stage 3: Battery Mounting (30–60 minutes)

The battery unit is mounted to the wall using heavy-duty brackets. Most residential batteries weigh 50–150 kg, so this requires at least two people and appropriate lifting equipment.

The installer will:
- Mark and drill mounting holes
- Secure the mounting bracket to the wall (into studs or masonry anchors)
- Lift and attach the battery unit to the bracket
- Verify the unit is level and secure

### Stage 4: Cabling (1–2 hours)

Cables are run from the battery to the switchboard. Depending on the installation, this may involve:
- Running cables through walls or ceiling cavities
- Installing conduit for cable protection
- Connecting DC cables (if DC-coupled with solar panels)
- Connecting AC cables to the switchboard

This is often the most time-consuming part of the installation, particularly if cable routes are complex.

### Stage 5: Commissioning (30–60 minutes)

Once everything is connected, the installer commissions the system. This involves:
- Powering up the battery management system
- Connecting the battery to your home Wi-Fi (for monitoring)
- Setting up the energy management software
- Running a test charge and discharge cycle
- Verifying that backup mode works correctly (the installer may briefly cut grid power to test this)

### Stage 6: Handover and Walkthrough (30 minutes)

A professional installer will walk you through the system before leaving. They should show you:
- How to monitor the battery state of charge (via app or display)
- How to check for alerts or errors
- What to do if the system goes into fault mode
- How to contact them for support

Ask for all documentation: the installation certificate, warranty cards, and any permit paperwork.

## How to Verify the Job Was Done Correctly

You don't need to be an electrician to do a basic quality check. Here's what to look for:

**Physical installation:**
- The battery is mounted securely and level — no wobble when you push it gently
- All cables are neatly routed and protected (not hanging loose or running across walkways)
- Cable entry points are sealed (no gaps where water or pests could enter)
- The battery display or indicator lights are showing normal status

**Documentation:**
- You have a signed installation certificate
- You have the warranty documentation for the battery
- You have any permit paperwork (if applicable in your jurisdiction)
- You have the installer's contact details for support

**Functionality:**
- The monitoring app is set up and showing live data
- The battery is charging (if solar panels are present and it's daylight)
- The backup circuit works (if you had one installed) — the installer should have tested this

**What to do if something seems wrong:**
Contact the installer immediately. Don't attempt to investigate electrical issues yourself. A reputable installer will return to address any issues promptly.

## After Installation: The First Week

**Day 1:** The battery will go through its first charge cycle. Monitor the app to see it charging from solar (or from the grid if solar isn't present). This is normal and expected.

**Days 2–7:** Watch the daily charge and discharge pattern. You should see the battery charging during the day (from solar or cheap overnight rates) and discharging in the evening. If the pattern looks wrong — for example, the battery is always empty or never charges — contact your installer.

**Week 2 onwards:** Check your electricity bill after the first full billing period. You should see a reduction in grid imports. The [BatteryBlueprint calculator](/calculator) can help you estimate what reduction to expect based on your system size and usage.

## FAQ

**Q: Do I need to be home the whole time?**

Yes. Someone over 18 must be present throughout the installation. The installer needs access to various parts of your home and will need your sign-off at key stages.

**Q: Will the power be off during installation?**

Yes, briefly. The installer will need to isolate your main power supply to work safely on the switchboard. This typically takes 1–3 hours. Plan accordingly — charge your phone, have a torch handy, and don't schedule anything that requires power during this window.

**Q: What if the installer finds additional work is needed?**

Get a written explanation and revised price before agreeing to any additional work. Legitimate additional work does occur (old switchboards, unexpected cable routing challenges), but you should understand exactly what's needed and why before authorising it.

**Q: How long will the battery last before needing replacement?**

Quality lithium batteries are warranted for 10 years and typically last 12–15 years before capacity degrades significantly. At end of life, the battery can usually be replaced without replacing the inverter or other system components.

**Q: What maintenance does a solar battery need?**

Very little. Lithium batteries are essentially maintenance-free. Keep the area around the battery clear, check the monitoring app periodically for alerts, and have the system inspected by a qualified electrician every 5 years or if you notice any issues. For a full breakdown of ongoing costs and what to budget for over the battery's lifetime, see our [solar battery cost guide](/cost/solar-battery-cost-uk-2026).

**Q: What should I do if the battery makes a noise or smells unusual?**

A faint hum from the inverter is normal. Any unusual sounds (clicking, buzzing, hissing) or smells (burning, chemical) are not normal. If you notice these, switch off the battery using the isolator switch (your installer will show you where this is) and contact your installer immediately. Do not attempt to investigate the battery yourself. Lithium batteries are very safe under normal conditions, but any sign of abnormal operation should be taken seriously.

**Q: How do I know if my installation was done to code?**

A properly certified installation will come with an Electrical Installation Certificate (UK), a permit sign-off (US), or an installation compliance certificate (Australia). If your installer didn't provide these documents, ask for them. In the UK, you can also check that the installation has been registered with your local building control authority — MCS-certified installers are required to do this automatically.

## Engineering Reality

Battery installation is a well-defined process, but the gap between average and exceptional installation quality has significant long-term consequences for system performance and warranty protection.

**Commissioning is not the same as installation.** Many homeowners assume that once the battery is mounted and the inverter is wired, the system is "done." The commissioning process — firmware verification, grid profile programming, protection relay testing, and initial charge-discharge cycle validation — is distinct from the physical installation and can take 2–4 hours. A sub-standard commissioning process that programs incorrect grid parameters can cause the inverter to trip unnecessarily during grid voltage fluctuations, reducing effective capacity utilisation by 10–30% in areas with variable grid voltage. Always confirm the commissioning report is provided at handover.

**Installation location affects long-term battery longevity more than any other single factor after chemistry.** LFP batteries are rated for operating temperatures of -10°C to 50°C, but their optimal range for maximum cycle life is 15°C to 35°C. A battery installed in a north-facing external garage in northern Scotland, where January temperatures regularly fall to -5°C, will operate within spec but may experience reduced charge acceptance at low temperatures. Some manufacturers (GivEnergy, Tesla) include a heating element in the BMS that activates at low temperatures — but only if the battery has sufficient SoC to power it. A battery discharged below 20% SoC in a -5°C garage may wake cold and fail to charge efficiently until the morning temperature rises. UK garage temperatures are acceptable for the majority of LFP products, but installation in fully heated internal spaces extends effective cycle life by a measurable margin.

**DNO notification delays can prevent grid export post-installation.** In the UK, battery systems connected to the grid must be notified to the Distribution Network Operator under G98 (systems ≤16A/phase, simplified notification) or G99 (larger systems, full application). G98 notifications are processed within 28 days. Until DNO notification is confirmed, the system should not export to the grid — operating in self-consumption-only mode. Some installers energise the system immediately post-installation and allow export before DNO confirmation, creating a regulatory non-compliance risk. Confirm with your installer that the system is configured for no-export mode until DNO notification is confirmed.

**Test the backup function explicitly — do not assume it works.** An installed system that is correctly commissioned for normal grid-tied operation may still have an incorrectly wired transfer switch that fails to operate correctly during a grid outage. The only way to confirm backup function is an intentional offline test — switching off the grid supply at the main isolator and confirming the battery takes over within the expected switchover time. This test should be conducted at handover, witnessed by the homeowner, and documented in the commissioning report. Installers who decline to perform this test at handover have not fully commissioned the backup function.

## When This Approach Breaks Down

The standard installation process is well-adapted to common residential properties. Several circumstances require non-standard approaches.

**Older consumer units with no space for additional circuits.** UK homes built before 2000 frequently have consumer units with all slots occupied. Battery installation requires at least one or two dedicated circuit positions — for the battery inverter and the critical loads subpanel. If the existing consumer unit has no available MCB slots, the installer must either add an extension consumer unit or replace the entire unit. This decision is made at site survey, but some installers only identify the conflict on the day of installation — causing delays and adder costs.

**Three-phase properties.** Most residential battery inverters are single-phase devices. A three-phase home with single-phase battery storage creates an inherent imbalance: the battery only offsets load on one of three phases. Energy drawn on the other two phases continues to attract grid import charges. For properties with three-phase meters where usage is distributed across all three phases, single-phase battery storage provides meaningfully less bill reduction than the raw kWh figures suggest. Three-phase battery systems exist (Tesla Powerwall 3, SMA Sunny Boy Storage) but carry a significant price premium.

**Part-time or holiday properties.** A battery installed in a property that is unoccupied for 3–4 months per year faces a specific calendar aging challenge. During extended vacancy periods, the battery should be stored at 50–60% SoC (not full and not empty) to minimise calendar aging — a requirement that most monitoring apps support via a "storage mode" setting but which requires active configuration by the homeowner. Homeowners who leave a holiday property without setting storage mode, with the battery fully charged, may experience 3–5% additional capacity loss per year compared to an actively managed system.

## Real-World Example

**Scenario:** A couple in Harpenden, Hertfordshire arranges a GivEnergy AIO 9.5 kWh installation in October 2025 via a regional MCS-certified installer. Installation day overview:

- 8:45 AM: Two-person installation team arrives with equipment
- 9:30 AM: Battery mounting bracket assessed — original quote assumed garage south wall, but utility meter is on south wall. Battery relocated to east wall. No additional charge.
- 11:15 AM: Cabling run complete. Grid isolation switch installed beside battery.
- 12:30 PM: Inverter wired and sealed. Lunch break.
- 12:50 PM: Commissioning starts. Firmware updated from V3.7 to V3.9 (Grid Compliance update required by DNO)
- 14:00 PM: Grid profile programmed to 50 Hz with G98 protection relay settings. Initial charge cycle initiated.
- 14:45 PM: Backup function tested by isolating grid supply. Inverter switched to island mode in 18ms. Homeowners confirmed lights remained on.
- 15:00 PM: Commissioning report completed and handed over. DNO G98 notification submitted.
- 15:15 PM: Monitoring app set up on homeowner's phone. All data visible and confirmed correct.

**Total installation time:** 6h15m (typical for first-time installation with firmware update)

**Post-installation note:** DNO G98 confirmation received 18 days after installation. System operated in self-consumption-only mode during this period. SEG export registration completed within 7 days of DNO confirmation.

**Lesson:** A correctly commissioned installation includes backup function testing, monitoring setup, and DNO notification — all conducted on installation day. If your installer leaves without testing the backup function and setting up monitoring, the installation is not complete. ## Engineering Recommendation

The installation experience directly predicts the long-term performance of your battery system. A high-quality installation by a qualified team results in a correctly commissioned, monitored, and protected asset. A poor installation creates problems that may only surface during an actual grid outage when backup protection is needed.

**Before installation day:**
- Confirm the installer has been to the property for a physical site survey — not simply an aerial photo or postcode lookup — before ordering equipment
- Confirm the commissioning scope in writing: firmware update, grid profile programming, backup function test, monitoring setup, and DNO notification submission
- Identify your preferred battery location in advance, and confirm with the installer whether that location has any conflicts with gas meters, fuse boxes, or ventilation requirements

**On installation day:**
- Be present for the handover — do not arrange installation while absent from the property
- Ask explicitly for a backup function test before the installation team leaves
- Confirm the monitoring app is working and showing live data before signing off

**After installation:**
- Register for SEG within 30 days of DNO notification confirmation — each month of delay is permanently lost export income
- Set a reminder for a 12-month system health check — use our [battery monitoring guide](/how-to/how-to-monitor-solar-battery-health) to understand what to look for

**The key decision trigger** is whether or not a backup function test was performed at handover. If it wasn't, the backup capability of the system is unverified and should be tested immediately. Use a safe method: isolate only the main incoming feed (not individual circuits) and confirm the battery holds voltage and continues powering the home. This test takes 5 minutes and is the single most important post-installation verification.
