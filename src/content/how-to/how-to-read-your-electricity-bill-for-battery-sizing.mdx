Most people have never actually read their electricity bill. They glance at the total, wince, and pay it. But if you want to size a solar battery correctly, your electricity bill contains the single most important number in the entire calculation: your **daily energy consumption in kilowatt-hours (kWh)**.

Get this number wrong and every other calculation falls apart. Overestimate and you'll spend thousands on capacity you'll never use. Underestimate and you'll run out of power at 9pm on a cold Tuesday.

This guide walks you through finding that number on any bill format — US, UK, or Australian — in under 10 minutes.

## What You're Looking For: The kWh Number

Your electricity bill charges you for energy in **kilowatt-hours (kWh)**. One kWh is the amount of energy used by a 1,000-watt appliance running for one hour. A typical electric kettle uses about 0.1 kWh per boil. A tumble dryer uses about 2.5 kWh per cycle.

Your bill will show your **total kWh consumed** over the billing period. This is the number you need.

**Do not use the dollar/pound/dollar amount.** Electricity prices vary wildly by location, time of day, and tariff type. The currency figure tells you what you paid, not how much energy you used. Battery sizing is about energy, not cost.

### Where to Find It

On most bills, look for a section called:
- "Energy Usage Summary"
- "Electricity Consumption"
- "Usage Details"
- "Meter Reading Summary"

It will show something like: **"Usage: 847 kWh"** or **"Units used: 847"**

That's your billing period total. Now you need to convert it to a daily average.

## Step 1: Find Your Billing Period Length

Bills are issued monthly, quarterly, or for a custom number of days. The billing period length is almost always printed near the meter reading dates.

Look for:
- "Service period: 15 Jan 2026 – 14 Feb 2026" → 30 days
- "Billing period: 90 days" → quarterly bill
- "Meter read dates: 01/12/2025 to 31/01/2026" → 61 days

Count the days between the start and end dates. Write this number down.

**Why it matters:** A 90-day bill showing 2,400 kWh looks alarming, but it works out to just 26.7 kWh/day — a fairly average UK home. A 30-day bill showing 800 kWh is 26.7 kWh/day too. Same house, same consumption, very different-looking numbers.

## Step 2: Calculate Your Daily Average

Divide your total kWh by the number of days in the billing period.

**Formula:** Daily kWh = Total kWh ÷ Number of days

**Examples:**
- 847 kWh over 30 days = **28.2 kWh/day**
- 2,400 kWh over 90 days = **26.7 kWh/day**
- 1,100 kWh over 45 days = **24.4 kWh/day**

This daily average is the primary input for the [BatteryBlueprint calculator](/calculator). Enter it in the "Daily Energy Load" field.

## Step 3: Adjust for Seasonal Variation

Here's the catch: one bill gives you one season. Summer consumption and winter consumption can differ by 30–50% in homes with electric heating or air conditioning.

**Best practice:** Pull 12 months of bills if you can. Most utility providers let you download usage history from their online portal. Calculate the daily average for each month, then:

- **For backup sizing:** Use your **highest month** (worst case). You want the battery to cover your peak demand.
- **For financial ROI sizing:** Use your **average across all months**. You're optimising for typical usage, not worst case.

If you only have one bill, note the season. A summer bill in Arizona will dramatically underestimate winter heating loads. A winter bill in Scotland will overestimate summer usage.

### The Seasonal Adjustment Rule of Thumb

If you only have one bill and you're in a temperate climate:
- **Summer bill:** Add 15–25% for winter heating loads
- **Winter bill:** Subtract 10–20% for summer average

These are rough guides only. The [battery sizing guide](/sizing/how-much-battery-storage-do-i-need) explains how to apply seasonal buffers properly using our calculator's winter mode toggle.

## Step 4: Account for Future Changes

Your current bill reflects your current lifestyle. But battery systems last 10–15 years. Think about what might change:

**Increases to plan for:**
- Electric vehicle (EV) charging: adds 10–30 kWh/day depending on mileage
- Heat pump installation: can add 5–20 kWh/day in winter
- Home office equipment: adds 1–3 kWh/day
- New family members or tenants

**Decreases to plan for:**
- LED lighting upgrades: saves 0.5–2 kWh/day
- New energy-efficient appliances
- Children leaving home

A common approach is to add 20% to your current daily average as a future-proofing buffer. This is more art than science, but it's better than sizing for today and running short in three years.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Mistake 1: Using the wrong unit.** Some older bills (particularly in the UK) show consumption in "units" where 1 unit = 1 kWh. This is fine — they're the same thing. But some gas bills show consumption in cubic metres or therms, which are completely different. Make sure you're reading the electricity section, not the gas section.

**Mistake 2: Using a single unusual month.** If you had guests staying for Christmas, or ran a portable air conditioner through a heatwave, that month's bill will be unrepresentative. Try to use at least 3 months of data, or flag the anomaly when calculating your average.

**Mistake 3: Confusing kW and kWh.** Kilowatts (kW) measure power — how fast energy is being used right now. Kilowatt-hours (kWh) measure energy — how much was used over time. Your bill shows kWh. Your battery is rated in kWh. Your solar panels are rated in kW. They're related but different. The [basics guide on kWh explained](/basics/kwh-explained) covers this distinction in detail.

**Mistake 4: Ignoring time-of-use tariffs.** If you're on a time-of-use (TOU) tariff where electricity costs more at peak hours, your bill may show separate kWh totals for peak and off-peak periods. For battery sizing, add them together to get your total daily consumption. The time-of-day split matters for financial optimisation, but not for capacity sizing.

## What to Do With Your Number

Once you have your daily kWh average, you're ready to size your battery. The [BatteryBlueprint calculator](/calculator) takes this number and applies engineering factors — depth of discharge, inverter efficiency, reserve buffer, and autonomy days — to calculate the actual battery capacity you need.

A quick reference for context:
- **Under 10 kWh/day:** Small home or apartment. A 5–10 kWh battery covers most backup needs.
- **10–20 kWh/day:** Average home. A 10–15 kWh battery for backup; 15–20 kWh for full daily coverage.
- **20–30 kWh/day:** Large home or EV charging. 15–25 kWh battery for backup; 25–40 kWh for full coverage.
- **Over 30 kWh/day:** High-consumption home. Consider a multi-battery system or commercial-grade storage.

These are rough guides. The calculator will give you a precise figure based on your specific inputs.

## FAQ

**Q: My bill shows "peak" and "off-peak" kWh separately. Which do I use?**

Add them together. For battery sizing purposes, you need your total daily consumption regardless of when it occurred. The peak/off-peak split is relevant for financial modelling (which hours to charge and discharge) but not for capacity sizing.

**Q: I'm on a smart meter — can I get hourly data?**

Yes, and it's much more useful than monthly totals. Most smart meter providers offer a web portal or app where you can download hourly or half-hourly consumption data. This lets you see exactly when you use the most energy, which helps you size for backup windows rather than just daily totals.

**Q: My bill is in therms or cubic metres. Is that my electricity?**

No — therms and cubic metres are gas units. Make sure you're reading the electricity section of your bill. If you have a combined gas and electricity bill, the electricity section will show kWh.

**Q: I don't have a bill yet — I just moved in. What should I use?**

Use the UK average (8–10 kWh/day for a 3-bedroom home) or US average (28–32 kWh/day) as a starting point, then adjust once you have 1–2 months of actual data. The [how much battery storage do I need](/sizing/how-much-battery-storage-do-i-need) guide has scenario-based estimates for different home sizes.

**Q: Should I use my highest bill or my average?**

It depends on your goal. For backup power (keeping the lights on during outages), use your highest month — you want to be covered in the worst case. For financial optimisation (maximising ROI from solar arbitrage), use your annual average — you're optimising for typical conditions, not extremes.

**Q: My bill shows a "demand charge" — what is that and does it affect battery sizing?**

Demand charges are common on commercial electricity tariffs but rare on residential ones. They charge you based on your peak power draw (measured in kW) during a billing period, not just your total energy consumption (kWh). If you have a demand charge on your residential bill, it means your utility is billing you for the highest 15- or 30-minute average power draw during the month. A battery can help reduce demand charges by smoothing out peak loads, but the sizing calculation is different from standard energy backup sizing. If you see a demand charge on your bill, mention it specifically when using the [BatteryBlueprint calculator](/calculator) — the approach to sizing changes.

**Q: How do I find historical usage data if I've lost old bills?**

Most utility providers store at least 12–24 months of usage history in your online account. Log in to your utility's customer portal and look for a section called "Usage History," "My Energy," or "Bill History." You can usually download a CSV or PDF of your monthly usage. In the UK, your smart meter data is accessible via your supplier's app or the national smart meter data portal. In Australia, your retailer's app typically shows 12 months of usage. If you can't access historical data online, call your utility's customer service line — they can usually provide historical usage data on request.

## Engineering Reality

Electricity bills provide essential battery sizing inputs, but the interpretation of bill data requires careful attention to what is measured, what is estimated, and what is missing from the information presented.

**Monthly kWh totals mask the daily and hourly patterns that actually drive battery sizing.** A utility bill stating "432 kWh in January" is an appropriate rough input for annual energy calculations. It is inadequate for battery sizing because it reveals nothing about when those 432 kWh were consumed. A household that runs the dishwasher, washing machine, and tumble dryer simultaneously at 8 PM draws 7–9 kW for 2 hours — a peak that determines whether a 3.6 kW inverter battery or a 7.6 kW inverter battery is required. Monthly bill data cannot reveal this. Smart meter half-hourly export data, downloadable from UK suppliers via the DCC portal, provides the granularity needed for accurate battery sizing. US customers can request 15-minute interval data from most utilities.

**Estimated versus actual reads affect sizing accuracy.** In the UK, approximately 18% of energy bills issued between 2022–2025 contained at least one estimated reading, according to Ofgem data. An estimated bill may understate or overstate actual consumption by 10–35% depending on the estimation algorithm used. Battery sizing based on an estimated bill produces a proportionally inaccurate result. Homeowners without smart meters who have received multiple consecutive estimated bills should request an actual meter reading before using bill data as a sizing input.

**Export revenue on bills does not indicate battery optimisation performance.** For existing solar owners, the SEG payment line on a UK bill shows total units exported times the SEG rate — not the volume of solar energy generated or consumed. A homeowner who exported 380 kWh in a quarter at 7p/kWh may have self-consumed 620 kWh and exported 380 kWh of the total 1,000 kWh generated. Alternatively, a poorly configured system may have exported 720 kWh and self-consumed only 280 kWh. Both scenarios produce similar generation totals but radically different self-consumption rates — and therefore different battery sizing requirements. Sizing a battery using export payment amounts without knowing the underlying generation and self-consumption split produces systematically incorrect results.

**Seasonal variation in UK bills is frequently larger than homeowners expect.** The ratio of UK summer to winter electricity consumption for a gas-heated home is typically 1:2.5 — monthly consumption in December/January is 2.5 times higher than in June/July. Battery sizing based on an average monthly bill misrepresents both extremes: it oversizes for summer (when a smaller battery would suffice) and undersizes for winter (when the full daily cycling capacity is needed). A proper sizing analysis should explicitly address both the summer and winter monthly totals as separate inputs.

## When This Approach Breaks Down

Bill-based sizing analysis is the appropriate starting point for most homeowners. It produces unreliable results in several specific circumstances.

**New builds with no billing history.** A newly built home has no electricity bill history. Sizing must be based on designed load estimates: number of occupants, appliance specifications from the house design specification, expected EV charging, and synthetic daily load profiles for the home's archetype. This is a legitimate engineering exercise but is fundamentally less accurate than actual bill data.

**Properties with dramatic occupancy changes.** A household where one high-consumption occupant recently moved out (a working-age child who moved to university) or where a new work-from-home arrangement has been implemented since the most recent billing year has a historical consumption profile that does not represent future consumption. In these cases, a 3–6 month window of recent bills post-change provides more relevant data than a full annual average.

**Properties with unmetered outbuildings or agricultural connections.** Properties billed at one meter for a house plus a farm building, detached annexe, or outbuilding on the same connection have a consumption total that includes non-domestic loads. Battery sizing based on the total metred consumption will oversize relative to the house's actual needs. Separate metering for the residential component provides the appropriate sizing input.

## Real-World Example

**Scenario:** A homeowner in Southampton downloads a year of smart meter data for battery sizing purposes. Annual total: 4,780 kWh. Simple monthly average: 398 kWh/month.

**On investigation of monthly data:**

| Month | kWh | Key driver |
|-------|-----|-----------|
| Jan | 620 kWh | Electric heating supplement, short days |
| Feb | 540 kWh | Cold month, still used supplement heating |
| Mar | 420 kWh | Shoulder month |
| Apr | 350 kWh | Spring, no heating |
| May | 270 kWh | Long days, low heating |
| Jun | 220 kWh | Longest days, no heating |
| Jul | 205 kWh | Peak summer |
| Aug | 215 kWh | Late summer |
| Sep | 290 kWh | Cooling shoulder |
| Oct | 410 kWh | Heating started again |
| Nov | 530 kWh | Winter arrived |
| Dec | 710 kWh | Shortest days, highest heating supplement |

**Summer daily average (Jun–Aug):** ~7.0 kWh/day → battery sizing for summer: 7–9 kWh (with 1.2× margin)
**Winter daily average (Nov–Jan):** ~19.4 kWh/day → battery sizing for winter cycle goals: 14+ kWh

**Conclusion:** A 9.5 kWh battery is adequate for summer full-cycle operation. In winter, it can only cover roughly 50% of daily consumption, cycling fully once per day. The homeowner chose a 9.5 kWh system specifically for summer optimisation, accepting that winter operation would be partial-cycle only — a financially correct decision because winter solar is insufficient to fill a larger battery anyway.

**Lesson:** Reading your bill to mean "I use 4,780 kWh/year, so I need a 4,780 ÷ 365 = 13.1 kWh battery" is incorrect. Seasonal analysis of monthly data reveals that summer self-consumption optimisation is achievable at lower battery capacity than winter full-cycle goals suggest. ## Engineering Recommendation

Electricity bills are the most accessible and legitimate primary source for battery sizing inputs. Extracting the right information from them requires going beyond the headline kWh total to examine seasonal variation, consumption timing, and actual versus estimated readings.

**From your utility bills, extract:**
1. 12–24 months of monthly kWh totals (actual readings preferred, not estimates)
2. Summer minimum (June–August average in Northern Hemisphere) and winter maximum (November–January average) monthly consumption separately
3. Any standing charges, TOU rate structure, or demand charges that affect which component of the bill a battery can actually reduce

**For more accurate half-hourly data (required for precise sizing):**
- UK: Request smart meter half-hourly data via your supplier's app or the DCC-approved portal (Bright, Hugo, Loop Energy)
- US: Request 15-minute interval data via the utility's Green Button download service (available from most major US utilities)
- Australia: Request interval data via your retailer's app or the Ausgrid/Endeavour data portal

**The key decision trigger** for bill-based versus smart-meter-data-based sizing is the degree of seasonal variation in your bills. If your highest monthly bill is more than 2× your lowest (common in most UK households), bill averages will underserve winter and overserve summer goals. Use seasonal monthly data as separate inputs to the [battery sizing calculator](/calculator) to generate summer-and-winter scenarios rather than a single annual average.
